NBT predictive algorithm & automation bot

Problem: During the pandemic, many students couldn't take the compulsory National Benchmark Test (NBT) for financial reasons, blocking them from registration.

Data analytics

Below is some of high impact analyses I conducted since Covid started:

Learning Management System analytics

During the Covid pandemic, I played a crucial role in monitoring and analyzing the online engagement of over 40,000 students at our institution. Through detailed tracking of student access, activity, video sessions, assessment completion/marks, and content behavior, I provided valuable insights that allowed management to make data-driven decisions during the uncertainty of the pandemic. My efforts resulted in the creation of 65 reports, which were used by top management to gain a better understanding of student engagement and learning outcomes. One of the reports were presented to the Minister of Higher Education by our rector.

Predictive analytics

Identifying students who are at risk of failing and calculating their risk profile. Using a variety of data sets, I have continuously analyzed student data to find leading indicators that allow our institution to proactively intervene before students fall behind. This has resulted in the creation of the innovative "No-Student-Left-Behind" algorithm, which is capable of accurately predicting which students are most likely to require additional support to succeed. Through rigorous cross-validation techniques, I have verified the efficacy of this algorithm, ensuring that it can effectively predict student performance and provide timely interventions to prevent academic failure.

A/B testing pilot

Conducted an experiment to analyze the effectiveness of nudging students to online support resources. Our group designed and implemented the pilot study, including the selection of the experimental groups and control groups, as well as the collection and analysis of data. The results provided valuable insights into which resources students respond best to, which informed the development of future interventions and support services.

Correlation study

Developed a framework for defining student success in higher education by conducted a correlation analysis to identify key factors that contribute to the definition of success. This study involved gathering and analyzing data from multiple sources as well as reviewing multiple definitions in literature. By identifying significant correlations, we were able to refine our definition of student success and create a more effective early warning system for at-risk students.

Outlier detection experiments

As the educational landscape constantly evolves, traditional rule-based approaches to nudging students may not always be effective. Through a series of outlier detection experiments, we were able to refine our credit load nudging approach by dynamically adjusting for outliers. By identifying patterns in students' operational data, we were able to create a more accurate and effective approach to nudging that accounts for unique student registration combinations.

Developmental modules impact analyses

I conducted a comprehensive impact analysis of developmental modules, evaluating their positive effects on the academic and non-academic experiences of students, particularly those from disadvantaged socio-economic backgrounds. This involved analyzing various data sets and identifying key performance indicators to assess the impact of the modules on student success. The findings were used to develop evidence-based recommendations to management. Each of these module annually generates more than R20 million in tuition fees for the University, making them financially beneficial. But more importantly, these modules have a direct positive impact on addressing the equity gap in our country by providing necessary support for students who come from disadvantaged backgrounds.

Artificial Intelligence

I am the lead data scientist for the Siyaphumelela project at the University of the Free State. The project objective I am responsible for is called "Investigate contextualised AI design, development and implementation in a higher education environment". Below is some of the main examples of work that I have done with artificial intelligence:

Internal Large Language Model

After the launch of ChatGPT late 2022, the University wanted to understand the potential impact of Generative Artificial Intelligence on higher education. I spent the first half of 2023 to create a small scale version of ChatGPT (more precisely a transformer) to understand the inner workings of Large Language Models. I used publicly available parallel corpora as well as a study guide from a module called "English Academic Literacy for Law" as data sets. The small LLM I created was fondly called "Baby AI" by my colleagues. The first experiment was translation. I trained the small LLM to translate the module from English to Afrikaans. The second experiment was question-and-answering. We asked the small LLM questions about the module and the lecturer helped evaluate whether it was true or not. We learned lessons about training time, data set size, accuracy and hallucinations. We also noticed the small LLM was obsessed with assessments. The lecturer and I discussed how we could improve the teaching and learning of the study guide instead of forcing students to only focus on assessments. I presented the findings at the 2023 SAAIR conference.

Generative AI for assessment research

Currently researching the application of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GAI) to support and streamline assessment processes in higher education. The project focuses on Critical Academic Literacy at the University of the Free State, investigating whether GAI can provide reliable, standardized, and timely feedback on written assessments. The project involves a comparative analysis of GAI-generated feedback against facilitator feedback, using thematic and textual analysis to evaluate adherence to analytic rubrics and its potential to reduce instructor workload. Our abstract has been accepted on 21 October 2025 by the South African Journal of Higher Education for their "AI in Higher Education in Global South" Special edition.

Prompt engineering & AI literacy workshops

Developed a prompt engineering crash course in the beginning of 2025 for the Center of Teaching and Learning as part of our internal capacity development plan. The prompt engineering guide was also turned into a workshop. The workshop I presented included AI literacy training and explaining the various algorithm to staff members. I was invited in May to Learner Analytics Institute to present a workshop on "AI-Driven Insights and implementation in Higher Education" where I shared best practices.

Presented AI workshop to the Council for Higher Education (CHE)

Presented AI workshop to the Council for Higher Education (CHE): The Council on Higher Education (CHE) is the steward of quality assurance in South Africa's higher education sector, a role that involves processing and analysing vast quantities of complex, text-rich data. From institutional accreditation documents and quality audit reports to policy papers and student data analysis, the data workload is immense. On 21 August 2025 the senior director and I presented a 4-hour introductory workshop designed to facilitate reflection but also develop practical skills to engineer prompts for the CHE context. The objective was to equip staff with the skills to utilise Large Language Models (LLMs) to explore how AI could enhance operational efficiencies, accelerate data analysis, and enhance decision-making processes, directly supporting the CHE's mandate.

AI stress testing & feasibility analysis

I ran several experiments to evaluate the feasibility to scale AI. I started off by evaluating paid vendors. The evaluation included stress testing API token and request limits. The stress tests revealed that it would be very expensive to roll out vendor based LLMs to the university's 2700+ modules. It was also difficult to track exact token (tokens used during "thinking" mode are not disclosed) use and it poses a risk because token use is tied to the payment structure. Next I moved on to a series of Open Source model testing. I picked a range of models (different authors, different parameter sizes) to conduct an open source LLM competency test. The competency test involved a logical reasoning, a coding, a decision making, a writing and an abstract conceptual synthesis test. These test gave us insight into model selection for scaling our project. The next part of the stress testing was to evaluate hardware limitations and optimizations (context length, GPU offload, parameter quantization).
